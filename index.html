Museum of Reconciliation

University Project

The museum route begins in a dark, narrow exhibition space known as the 'Conflict Wall', and then transitions into a brighter Heritage exhibition area. It culminates in a high, fully exposed room representing the feeling of reconciliation.

This journey, moving from darkness to light, symbolizes the path from conflict to reconciliation and demonstrates how light serves as an essential architectural element.

Urban Acupuncture - Mina, Tripoli

University Project

Urban acupuncture is a socio-environmentaltheory that combines contemporary urban design with traditional Chinese acupuncture, using small-scale interventions to transform the larger urban context. Sites are selected through analysis of aggregate social, economic and ecological factos, and are developed through a dialogue between designers and the community. Just as the practice of acupuncture is aimed at relieving stress in the human body, the goal of urban acupuncture is to relieve stress in the built environment.

The main goal of the proposal is to reunite Tripoli with the neglected Mina coastline, and extend the life of the city to the waterfront in some specific points. This would be achieved through operations of urban regenaration, adjusted to the existing and to the specific cultural, sociogical and economical context. The proposal concentrates visual impact, identity and activity along side of the corniche, while spreading them into the tissue of the area of intervention.

Water towers at Tripoli / The morphology of in-between spaces

University Project

One of the biggest frustrations in our urban cities is POLLUTION. I studied and analyzed Tripoli, that is one of Lebanon’s immensely dense and urban city and I found out that it suffers mostly from water pollution due to the highly polluted Abu Ali river flowing through it. So I thought why not create a water treatment system for this river water and for rainwater. By creating this equipment in some of Tripoli’s residual, unused spaces between building I can not only give value to those useless spaces but also to Tripoli itself. I chose to work on three linear voids located in the Riad Salemeh Street that is centered in Tripoli, is a commercial and residential street.

I was inspired by the most important natural mechanism that absorbs and treats water by its roots, branches and leafs; the tree. And what inspired me the most was pictures of the roots of trees planted on sidewalks, following the voids between the sidewalk’s tiles. So at first, I thought about implanting a center piece in my voids that will be the trunk of the project and I started drawing horizontal vectors following the shape of the void that will later on be the pipes absorbing the river water from the ground. As for the vertical vectors, pipes will be following them ascending from the ground for the river water and descending from above for collecting rainwater. Then, I took the configuration of one of the voids I picked and sliced it into smaller shapes. I then realized that separating those smaller shapes lead me to infinite possibilities of new configurations and modules. Those infinite possibilities will help in creating the volumes I need for the project and will later on help with creating an everlasting tower that keeps on growing and adapting to the city’s needs. These infinite modules will surround the core and trunk of the project and will be held and joined by the branches of the system that are the pipes. Those pipes will therefore be the structure of the project.
